<p>Every paper has a story behind it. That story is like a cake. It usually has a few layers of science, several layers of people, a lot of emotions mixed into the batter, and the frosting on top is the acceptance, publication and recognition it gets. Good papers don‚Äôt always feel like they are going to end up like that, even if you think the recipe is good, you always wonder what will come out of the oven. This is the first in a series of ‚Äúbehind the paper‚Äù war stories ‚Äì sharing the mess in the kitchen behind the sweet taste of my publications. </p>
<p>Paper: <a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2007.00394">https://arxiv.org/abs/2007.00394</a> (accepted to WACV 2021<s> in Hawaii </s>online üôÅ )<br/>Project website: <a href="https://ikeaasm.github.io/">https://ikeaasm.github.io/</a></p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">How it all began</h3>
<p>The story begins in July 2019. I just arrived in Australia with my wife and two kids (2yo, 6yo), fresh out of my PhD at the Technion (Isreal). I was super excited and motivated to start my first ‚Äúgrown-up‚Äù job as a research fellow at the <a href="https://www.anu.edu.au/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">ANU </a>node of <a href="https://www.roboticvision.org/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">ACRV</a>.  I met Steve (<a href="http://users.cecs.anu.edu.au/~sgould/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">Prof. Stephen Gould</a>), my supervisor, and we walked to the campus outdoor coffee shop. It was freezing but I was fired up from the project he described. We were going to build a perception system that will help people assemble IKEA furniture. At the time, I  had a few IKEA furniture in my apartment that I had to put together more than once because I put one of the pieces the wrong way, so it was an easy sell.   The general idea was to create something holistic that will capture and extract as much information as we can ‚Äì multi-view RGB images, Depth images, 3D point clouds, human poses, hand poses, part poses, geometry, actions, CAD models,  human-object interactions, track everything and everyone<s>, estimate what they had for breakfast and why are they so grumpy</s>. I‚Äôm sure Steve‚Äôs list was shorter and well defined, this is how it felt in my head. I was about to be overwhelmed with the amount of work coming my way when Steve mentioned the team.   </p>
<p></p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Meeting the team</h3>
<p>The team includes Xin (<a href="https://cecs.anu.edu.au/people/xin-yu" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">Dr. Xin Yu</a>), Fatemeh (<a href="https://fatemeh-slh.github.io/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">Dr. Fatemeh Saleh</a>), Dylan (<a href="https://sites.google.com/view/djcampbell" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">Dr. Dylan Campbell</a>), Cristian (soon to officially be <a href="https://crodriguezo.github.io/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">Dr. Cristian Rodriguez</a>), Hongdong (<a href="http://users.cecs.anu.edu.au/~hongdong/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">Prof. Hongdong Li</a>), and Steve (<a href="http://users.cecs.anu.edu.au/~sgould/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">Prof. Stephen Gould</a>). The first time we all were in one room was the first ‚ÄúHuman, Robots, and Actions‚Äù project meetings, but, mostly we were two doors away from each other. </p>

<p>The capturing set up was simple yet annoying. It included a huge tripod with 2 Kinects (top and front view) and an additional tripod with another Kinect (side view) and Xin‚Äôs computer. Sounds like not too bad. but in practice, there were so many cables.   Speaking of annoying, we initially wanted to use Microsoft‚Äôs SDK but soon discovered that it is impossible to capture feeds from multiple sensors symultaniousely so moved on to using <a href="https://github.com/OpenKinect/libfreenect2" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">libfreenect</a>2 that didn‚Äôt have all of the SDK features implemented. We also wanted to capture at a high frame so we prioritized most of the processing to be done off-line.</p>
<p> Xin and I checked the file names, frame rate, camera feeds, pushed to git, fixed bugs, did more trial runs, pushed to git, fixed some more issues, pushed to git, scheduled people to assemble, documented, pushed to git (Steve loves git). Fatemeh, Dylan and Cristian helped us pre-assemble fasteners, carry the capturing setup and furniture parts around the building, and disassemble the furniture (someone had to disassemble all of the furniture that people assembled). Sometimes I think we could have also created the ‚ÄúIKEA furniture disassembly dataset‚Äù at no additional cost. </p>
<p>We were finally ready. We spent the next two weeks collecting about 200 furniture assembly videos. We collected all day every day. We even collected data at the ACRV‚Äôs major events like <a href="https://www.roboticvision.org/rvss-2020/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">RVSS </a>and <a href="https://www.roboticvision.org/rv_event/robovis-2019/">RoboVis</a>, where we got all of the big Profs to assemble furniture (even <a href="https://staff.qut.edu.au/staff/peter.corke" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">Prof. Peter Corke</a>, the centre‚Äôs director). </p>

<p>A few hours later, I got the text. </p>
<p>My eyes teared up when I read it. I went through all of the stages of greaf. First I was in denial, I thought someone is playing a prank. Then I was angry, mostly at myself ‚Äì how did we miss it? Finally, I accepted reality ‚Äì due to a glitch in the capture setup we were only able to capture one depth stream instead of three. It was the lowest low. </p>
<p> I vividly remember the next morning. Steve came into my office. Clearly, he didn‚Äôt know yet. He was still smiling. I told him. He was still smiling and said ‚ÄúCome on‚Ä¶ really?‚Äù (denial). He skipped the angry stage (as far as I could tell) and after a few questions of trying to understand how it happened and what we do have, he accepted it. I know one thing. The way Steve responded to the bad news was an inspiration to us all. Its like he knew how badly we all felt and understood that the right thing to do was to pick up the pieces. A true leader (I know he is probably reading this. I am not kissing ass. It was truly a special moment).       </p>
<p>The team‚Äôs reaction was also amazing. No one blamed anyone. Everyone was supportive. I remember thinking how lucky I was to be a part of this amazing team. The decision was to accept the imperfection and continue with what we have to the next step.</p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Put a label on it</h3>
<p>The next months were focused on labelling the data. Dylan was in charge of human pose, Fatemeh on segmentation and tracking, and I took on the action annotations. Cristian was the teams ‚Äúgo-to mechanical turk‚Äù extraordinaire, and Xin got a position at UTS in Sydney (bye Xin, we still miss you!).</p>
<p> Labelling took a lot of time. We built labelling tools, got students to label, screened turkers, validating annotations, creating pseudo ground truths by overfitting the data, corrected labels, validated again, until‚Ä¶ </p>
<p>It was finally ready. </p>

<p>We set a goal to submit to WACV 2021 (who doesn‚Äôt want a fully-funded trip to Hawaii?). I created a shared overleaf latex document and everyone poured their content in. </p>
<p>We subdivided the dataset into train-test sets. We trained SOTA models as benchmarks. Then we subdivided it again (after realizing that my subdivision code had a bug in it). Then we trained the models again, and then again (to fix the parameters to be as reported in the papers). I really wanted to squeeze in a new action recognition method that can use all of the data streams together, there was just not enough time.   </p>
<p>We submitted the paper. </p>
<p>I remember the relief I felt when clicking the ‚Äúsubmit‚Äù button. At that point, I couldn‚Äôt see the beauty and contribution in the dataset, only its compromises and flaws. </p>
<h3 class="wp-block-heading">Every end is a new beggining</h3>
<p>I think one of the things I enjoyed most about this project is the comradery and bonding that happened between the members of the team. Personally, it was also a great way to get to know everyone ‚Äì no need for a conversation starter if you can simply ask ‚ÄúHey, did I get you to assemble some furniture yet?‚Äù. It was clear to me that the submission of the paper was one of those ‚Äúevery end is a new beginning‚Äù moments. The IKEA ASM dataset paper was done, but it was the starting point for (hopefully) many more papers to come. </p>
<p></p>
<p>The paper was accepted.</p>
<p></p>
<p>Remember: Your papers are like your children, even if they are annoying sometimes you love them all.</p>
